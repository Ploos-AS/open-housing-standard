# Appendix A — Design Rationale

**Status:** Draft

**Informative**

---

## A.1 Purpose

This appendix explains the rationale behind the design principles of the Open Housing Standard (OHS).

It is informative and does not define normative requirements.

---

## A.2 Long Service Life

OHS assumes that residential buildings should remain useful for multiple generations.

A design service life of at least **100 years** encourages decisions that prioritize durability, maintainability, and adaptability over short-term cost optimization.

The objective is not merely to maximize structural lifetime, but to ensure that buildings remain desirable, maintainable, and economically viable throughout their service life.

---

## A.3 Open Standards

Buildings typically outlive the companies that design them, the software used to model them, and the technologies installed within them.

For this reason, OHS prioritizes open standards and openly documented formats whenever practical.

Open standards reduce vendor lock-in, improve interoperability, and increase the likelihood that future generations can access building documentation.

---

## A.4 Vendor Neutrality

The purpose of OHS is to describe *what* should be achieved rather than *which* products should be used.

Specifications therefore avoid dependence upon individual manufacturers whenever equivalent technical solutions exist.

Vendor neutrality encourages competition, innovation, and long-term maintainability.

---

## A.5 Repairability

Buildings inevitably require maintenance.

OHS therefore favors designs where individual components may be inspected, repaired, upgraded, or replaced without unnecessary demolition.

Repairability contributes directly to sustainability by extending the useful life of the building.

---

## A.6 Adaptability

The needs of occupants change over time.

Family size, accessibility requirements, technology, and regulations evolve.

Buildings should therefore accommodate foreseeable change with minimal structural intervention.

Adaptability extends the useful life of a dwelling while reducing lifecycle cost.

---

## A.7 Technical Separation

Mechanical, electrical, plumbing, communication, and automation systems typically evolve much faster than the structural building envelope.

Separating building services from structural elements allows future upgrades without compromising the building itself.

The concept of a Technical Core supports this objective.

---

## A.8 Documentation

Future owners should not have to rediscover how a building was designed.

Complete documentation improves maintenance, renovation, emergency response, and long-term stewardship.

Documentation is therefore considered an essential part of the building rather than an optional deliverable.

---

## A.9 Accessibility

Accessibility benefits everyone.

Designing for varying physical abilities improves usability throughout the entire service life of the building.

Features originally intended for accessibility frequently provide broader long-term value.

---

## A.10 Sustainability

OHS views sustainability primarily as longevity.

Buildings that remain useful, maintainable, and desirable for many decades generally consume fewer resources than buildings requiring premature replacement.

Repairability, adaptability, and durability are therefore considered key sustainability principles.

---

## A.11 Interoperability

Buildings increasingly contain software, digital models, sensors, automation systems, and communication infrastructure.

Open interfaces and documented behavior reduce integration costs and improve long-term flexibility.

Interoperability also supports independent implementation by multiple vendors.

---

## A.12 Future Readiness

No standard can predict future technologies.

Instead, OHS encourages designs that preserve future choice.

Providing spare capacity, accessible service routes, modular building services, and open documentation allows buildings to evolve over time without unnecessary reconstruction.

---

## A.13 Simplicity

Whenever multiple solutions satisfy the same objective, OHS generally favors the simpler solution.

Simplicity reduces construction complexity, maintenance effort, lifecycle cost, and implementation risk.

---

## A.14 Human-Centred Design

Buildings exist for people.

Technical systems, construction methods, and digital infrastructure should support the needs of occupants rather than dictate them.

Throughout OHS, human usability takes precedence over technological novelty.

---

## A.15 Continuous Improvement

The Open Housing Standard is intended to evolve.

Experience from practical implementations, research, and community feedback will inform future revisions.

This appendix explains the current design philosophy but does not limit future improvements made through the governance process.

---

## A.16 Philosophy

The Open Housing Standard is founded on a simple premise:

> A home should become more valuable through good maintenance,
> thoughtful adaptation, and open documentation—not obsolete because
> of proprietary technology, inaccessible construction, or changing trends.

The purpose of OHS is therefore not merely to define buildings,
but to encourage homes that remain practical, maintainable,
and desirable for generations.
